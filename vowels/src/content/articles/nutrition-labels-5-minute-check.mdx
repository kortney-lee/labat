## A 5-Minute Method for Reading Labels

### 1. Start with the Serving Size

- **Why it Matters**: The serving size? It's the cornerstone of all nutrient information on the label. Misunderstanding this can lead to consuming more calories and nutrients than intended. Wild, right? Portion distortion is a common issue that can significantly mess with your calorie and nutrient intake.
- **Practical Tip**: Always compare the serving size with what you actually eat. If you're munching on double the serving size, donâ€™t forget to double the calories and nutrients too.

### 2. Check Total Calories

- **Why it Matters**: Knowing the calorie content is key for managing your weight and energy intake. For an average adult, the daily caloric intake is around 2,000 to 2,500 calories, but it can vary based on age, sex, and activity level.
- **Practical Tip**: Use this information to help maintain balance with your daily calorie goals. You donâ€™t want to overdo it or cut yourself short.

### 3. Focus on Key Nutrients

- **Nutrients to Limit**: Watch out for saturated fat, trans fat, cholesterol, and sodium. Consuming too much of these bad boys can lead to chronic diseases like heart disease and hypertension.
- **Nutrients to Get Enough Of**: Make sure you're getting enough dietary fiber, protein, vitamins, and minerals. These are your body's best friends, supporting overall health and reducing the risk of chronic diseases.
- **Practical Tip**: Aim for higher percentages of Daily Values (DVs) for good nutrients and lower percentages for those you should limit.

### 4. Look for Added Sugars

- **Why it Matters**: Excessive added sugars? A recipe for weight gain and an increased risk of heart disease. The American Heart Association recommends limiting added sugars to 25 grams per day for women and 36 grams for men.
- **Practical Tip**: Opt for products with little to no added sugars. If youâ€™re craving something sweet, go for natural sources like fruits.

### 5. Assess the Ingredient List

- **Why it Matters**: Ingredients are listed by weight, meaning the first few items make up most of the product. Knowing what's in your food helps you spot products with whole foods and minimal processed ingredients.
- **Practical Tip**: Focus on the first five ingredients. Seek out whole grains and natural ingredients, and avoid products where sugars or hydrogenated oils are listed at the top.
